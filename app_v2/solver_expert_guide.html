1 · The anatomy of an optimization model — the expert track

This section is the "turn me an expert" part. It uses our real formulations as the textbook, because the fastest way to internalize this material is to see one production-grade model per concept rather than toy examples.

1.1 Decision variables, objective, constraints — the three-part contract

Every optimization model — from a two-product blend to Gurobi's largest airline crew schedule — is exactly three things:

① Decision variables

The numbers the solver is allowed to choose. Everything else is a parameter (data you fixed). The single most common modeling bug in industry is confusing the two — hard-coding a quantity that should have been a variable, or exposing as a variable something the business has already decided.

② Objective function

One scalar expression of the variables to maximize or minimize. One. When stakeholders want several ("max service AND min cost"), you either price one into the other's units (our approach: stockout risk is priced in ₹ inside transport's ranking) or you sweep a weight and show the frontier. A solver never "balances" goals it wasn't given a price for.

③ Constraints

Equations/inequalities the chosen numbers must satisfy. Two species: physics (inventory balance: what you carry = what you had + made − sold) and policy (safety stock floor ≥ target). Physics constraints are never negotiable; policy constraints are where all the interesting duals live (§1.3).

The contract

"Find values of ① that optimize ② subject to ③." If all expressions are linear and variables continuous, it's an LP. Add integers — MILP. That one-word change alters the computational universe (§1.4).

Worked example — the product-mix engine (profitmix.py)

Our profit maximizer is the cleanest live specimen. Stripped to its core (profitmix.py:57, pulp.LpProblem("Profit_Maximizer", LpMaximize)):

max  Σk margink · qk
s.t.  Σk assigned to line l (cyclek/60) · qk ≤ available_hoursl   ∀ lines l  (capacity physics)
      qk + Σ cannibalization terms ≤ demand_ceilingk   ∀ SKUs k  (market reality)
      qk ≥ 0

Three expert details hiding in that innocuous model, all real in our code:

  • The ceiling is a modeling decision, not data. profitmix.py computes demand_ceiling differently per demand mode — MTS uses the consensus forecast, MTO uses booked orders only, ATO blends — because "how much could we sell" is a different question per business model. The constraint encodes a belief about the world.
  • Cannibalization couples variables. A new SKU's sales eat a sibling's ceiling (profitmix.py:306: q[k] + lpSum(cannib_terms) ≤ effective_ceiling). Without that coupling the solver would happily "sell" the same customer twice. Coupling constraints are where models earn their keep — anything without them is a spreadsheet.
  • Cost semantics are a contract. The same engine runs three labor-cost modes (per_unit / hourly / salaried_idle, profitmix.py:42) because charging salaried labor per-unit double-counts it. Most real-world "the solver is wrong" complaints are actually the objective priced the world wrong.

1.2 The geometry: why linear programs are easy

Each linear constraint slices space with a flat plane; the surviving region (the feasible region) is a convex polytope — a crystal with flat faces. A linear objective is a tilted plane over it. Tilt a plane over a crystal and the highest point is always at a vertex (corner). That single geometric fact is the entire reason LPs with millions of variables solve in seconds:

  • Simplex walks vertex → adjacent better vertex until no neighbour improves. Convexity makes the local optimum global — there is nowhere better hiding elsewhere.
  • Interior-point / barrier methods cut through the middle instead; better on huge sparse LPs. Commercial solvers run both concurrently and take the first finisher.
The app ships a live version of this picture: the VIS-6 feasible-region toy (Demand tab, V3-3) is a true 2-SKU restriction of the live profitmixPayload with exact vertex enumeration — the golden corner equals the demand ceilings.

Expert consequences of vertex-optimality:

  • Solutions are extreme. An LP will load one line to 100.000% and leave another at 31% because some corner is optimal — it will never "spread things sensibly" unless smoothness is priced or constrained. If a plan looks knife-edged, that's the geometry, not a bug.
  • Degeneracy: when more than the minimum number of constraints meet at the optimal vertex, many equally-optimal solutions exist and tiny input changes flip which one you get. This is why two byte-identical-cost plans can look different — and why our byte-identity gate tests (V5-3/V5-4 toggle-off) matter: they prove the inputs didn't move, not just the cost.
  • Sensitivity is local. Shadow prices (§1.3) are slopes at the current vertex; they hold only within a range. "One more hour is worth ₹412" is true for the next few hours, not the next thousand.

1.3 Duality and shadow prices — the half of LP most people never learn

Every LP has a twin. If the primal asks "what's the best plan given my resources?", the dual asks "what is each resource worth?" Formally, the dual assigns a price yi ≥ 0 to every constraint and seeks the cheapest set of prices that explains away all profit. At the optimum the two meet exactly (strong duality): best plan value = total imputed resource value. Nothing is left unexplained.

The three working tools duality gives a planner

ToolMeaningWhere it lives in our system
Shadow price (dual value, π) ₹ change in the objective per unit relaxation of a constraint's right-hand side. A non-binding constraint has π = 0 — slack resources are worth nothing at the margin. aggregate.py:364–380 reads c.pi off every capacity row, flags binding when slack < 0.01, and returns a shadow_prices list — the Plan tab can say "one more regular-capacity hour in P3 is worth ₹X". This is the hook GAP-5 uses to justify capacity investment.
Complementary slackness Either a constraint is tight or its price is zero — never both loose. It's the formal version of "you only pay for what binds." Implicit in the binding filter: we drop rows where |π| < 1e-6 and slack exists, because they carry no information.
Reduced cost For a variable stuck at zero: how much its objective coefficient must improve before the solver would use it. "Your margin on SKU-X must rise ₹37/unit before it enters the mix." profitmix.py:563 surfaces precisely this idea in words: "At $N+, margin/hr exceeds marginal product" — a reduced-cost explanation of why a SKU is excluded and what price flips it.
The expert gotcha almost everyone trips on Integer programs do not have valid duals. Once branch-and-bound fixes binaries, the ".pi" values a solver reports reflect the final node's LP relaxation, not a true sensitivity of the integer optimum. Our capital.py:69–133 handles this correctly and documents why: it re-solves the model with x relaxed to continuous to read a meaningful budget shadow price ("marginal NPV per extra ₹ of CapEx"). Gurobi has the same limitation — their docs tell you the same workaround. Any tool showing you "MIP shadow prices" without this caveat is lying to you.

Why this matters commercially: shadow prices are the difference between a solver that says "here's the plan" and one that says "here's the plan, and the binding constraint is paint-line hours, and relaxing it is worth ₹412/hr, so the ₹18L second paint line pays back in 9 months." The second sentence is what executives buy. Section 6 audits how much of our system speaks it.

1.4 Integrality — where the cliff is

The moment a variable must be whole (a worker hired, an MOQ triggered, a line changed over), the feasible region shatters from one convex crystal into a lattice of disconnected points. Vertex-walking no longer works. The universal algorithm is branch & bound:

1
Relax — solve the LP ignoring integrality. Its value is a bound: the integer answer can never beat it.
2
Branch — pick a fractional variable (x=3.4), split the world: one subproblem with x≤3, one with x≥4.
3
Bound & prune — solve each subproblem's relaxation; if its bound is worse than the best integer solution found so far (the incumbent), discard the entire subtree unexamined.
4
The gap — at any moment: (best bound − incumbent)/incumbent. Gap 0% = proven optimal. "Optimality" in Gurobi's marketing means exactly this proof, not a vibe.

Pruning power is everything. Commercial solvers win on presolve (shrinking the model before solving), cutting planes (extra valid inequalities that tighten relaxations), and branching heuristics — that's the actual content of the Gurobi-vs-CBC gap in §3.

1.5 Linearization — the craft of staying linear

LP/MILP solvers only eat linear expressions. The craft is reformulating the nonlinear world without losing exactness — or knowing precisely what you lost. Our codebase demonstrates the four canonical moves:

  • Split absolute values into two non-negatives. Inventory-vs-backorder: aggregate.py carries It ≥ 0 and Bt ≥ 0 with balance (I[t] − B[t]) == carried + made − demanded. Because both cost money, the solver never holds both at once — the split is exact because the objective penalizes both sides. (That condition is the part people forget.)
  • Big-M / indicator linking. MOQ: q ≤ M·y and q ≥ moq·y with binary y. Expert rule: make M as small as logically possible (we bound by demand-horizon need, not 10⁹) — loose Ms wreck branch-and-bound's pruning because relaxations go meaningless.
  • One-hot enumeration of a bounded integer domain. The meio.py √τ trick above — exact, not piecewise.
  • Expected-value aggregation, repaired downstream. True sequence-dependent changeovers need w[k₁,k₂,l,t] binaries — a TSP inside every line-week. production.py deliberately charges switch-count × mean changeover instead (comment at production.py:~222: "expected-value average over eligible pairs"), keeping the MILP small; then sequencing.py (GAP-8) repairs the lost structure post-hoc: exact brute force ≤ 8 SKUs (8! = 40,320 paths), nearest-neighbour + node-reinsertion local search above. That two-stage decomposition — coarse exact model, fine heuristic repair — is how real APS systems work, and we say so in the docstring rather than pretending the MILP sequenced anything.

1.6 Uncertainty — expectation, tails, and what "robust" actually means

Everything above optimizes one assumed future. Three escalating responses to that lie:

① Simulation (evaluate, don't optimize) — montecarlo.py

Take the committed plan, redraw the world N times — demand ~ N(forecast, mape·forecast) truncated at 0, material cost shocks, lead-time jitter — and report the distribution of profit and service. V4-7 made demand and cost shocks bivariately correlated via Cholesky of [[1,ρ],[ρ,1]] with default ρ=0.4 (montecarlo.py:51–59): when demand booms, input prices tend to rise too, so independent draws understate tail risk — a subtle, very real modeling error we fixed. Monte Carlo never chooses anything; it grades choices.

② The newsvendor critical fractile (optimize the expectation analytically)

Order Q* such that  P(demand ≤ Q*) = cunder / (cunder + cover)

The single most important closed-form result in inventory theory: the optimal service level is a cost ratio, not a preference. A ₹40 stockout vs ₹10 holding ⇒ stock to the 80th percentile. Every safety-stock formula (z·σ·√LT in policy.py and pattern_sensing.py) is this idea with a Gaussian assumption.

③ CVaR — optimize the tail itself — cvar.py

Expectation-optimal can still mean "ruined in the bad 5%". Conditional Value-at-Risk minimizes the average of the worst (1−β) of outcomes. The Rockafellar–Uryasev construction makes this an LP, and ours is a faithful implementation (cvar.py:47–62):

min  α + 1/((1−β)·S) · Σ_s z_s          # α converges to the VaR; z_s the tail excess
s.t. z_s ≥ (h·o_s + p·u_s) − α   ∀s     # loss beyond α in scenario s
     o_s ≥ Q − d_s,  u_s ≥ d_s − Q      # overage / underage linearization (§1.5)

The decision variable α becomes the Value-at-Risk at the optimum — the model discovers its own risk threshold. The resulting Q* feeds procurement.py as a CVaR-robust safety-stock floor (ss_source='cvar') — simulation-informed optimization, not just simulation. The honest hierarchy: deterministic LP < simulation-graded LP < tail-optimized LP; full multi-stage stochastic programming (recourse decisions inside the optimization) is the next rung and is NOT BUILT — see §8 for where it would matter.

Regime awareness — risk.py

A 2-state Gaussian HMM classifies a cost/price series into LOW-VOL vs HIGH-VOL regimes. The point for a planner: σ is not stationary. A safety stock sized on blended history is wrong in both regimes — too fat in calm, too thin in storm. Regime detection tells you which σ to feed the newsvendor math today.

1.8 Forecasting and sensing — the input layer that decides everything

Optimal against a wrong forecast is precisely wrong. Our input layer (forecast.py + pattern_sensing.py) embodies four principles worth internalizing:

  • Model tournaments beat model loyalty. Eight families (naive-seasonal baseline, Holt-Winters add/mult, AIC-gridded ARIMA, RandomForest, GradientBoosting, XGBoost-if-present, MLP, and a Holt-Winters+ML-residual hybrid) compete on holdout MAPE and the leaderboard wins — never a favourite model. The naive baseline's presence is the integrity check: any model that can't beat "same as last season" should be fired, and the leaderboard makes that visible.
  • Refuse to overfit, out loud. With history < 18 periods the engine declines to blend and says why (forecast.py:708: "history < 18 periods — a blend would overfit the holdout"). A forecaster that knows its ignorance is rarer than one with a lower MAPE.
  • Exogenous signals only where they can work. V4-4 price/footfall regressor columns feed ML and hybrid models only — classical models structurally can't consume them, and the code says so instead of silently ignoring the columns.
  • Sensing ≠ forecasting. pattern_sensing.py (the SAP-IBP-style five pieces) owns weeks 0–6 by pattern-matching recent residuals against a template library (promo lift, holiday ramp, weekend dip, post-outage bounce, trend-break) via cosine similarity, hands off to the statistical forecast at 7+ with a soft blend, and propagates a posterior σ into safety stock. Short horizon is a recognition problem; long horizon is an extrapolation problem.

And the governance teeth: consensus commit is gated when MAPE > 30% (V3-2, enforced in the Demand tab) — a forecast too wrong to plan on is stopped at the door rather than laundered through five downstream solvers.

1.9 Reading a solve like an expert

  • Status is a contract. Optimal = proven (to gap). Infeasible = your constraints contradict — the model is telling you about reality (demand physically exceeds capacity × the fence). Not Solved / time-limited = best-found-so-far; aggregate.py:262 runs timeLimit=30, so a monster instance returns an incumbent, not a certificate. Treat the three differently or get burned.
  • Infeasibility is information you must localize. CBC cannot compute an IIS (irreducible infeasible subsystem — the minimal clashing constraint set); Gurobi can (§3). The poor-man's substitute we already use structurally: elastic variables — backorder Bt and lost-sales Lt in aggregate.py are priced escape valves that convert "INFEASIBLE" into "feasible, and look how expensive your demand plan is." Elasticizing hard constraints and reading which valves opened is the single best infeasibility-debugging trick on a free solver.
  • Question the inputs before the optimizer. Our UNITS.md ledger exists because the two historical "solver bugs" worth a post-mortem were a percent-vs-fraction confusion (yield_pct is a FRACTION despite the name) and an hours-vs-minutes mismatch — both input contracts, neither the mathematics. This generalizes: ~90% of "the optimizer is wrong" in industry is payload semantics.
  • Watch what binds, not what's chosen. The plan is the output; the binding constraint set is the diagnosis. Two plans can look similar while their binding sets reveal one is capacity-starved and the other demand-starved — opposite investments.

3 · The solver landscape — CBC vs HiGHS vs Gurobi vs Hexaly vs CP-SAT, honestly

First, terms: PuLP is not a solver. It's a Python modeling layer that writes the model file and shells out to a solver — ours is CBC (COIN-OR Branch & Cut, open source). "Is Gurobi better than PuLP" mixes layers; the real question is CBC vs Gurobi, and the answer depends entirely on scale and structure.

Where CBC genuinely falls short for us, today

  • Scale headroom, not current pain. Our largest instance — multi-site procurement.py with node caps, or production.py at full SKU×line×week — is thousands of variables; CBC solves the whole golden path in seconds. The timeLimit=30 guard on aggregate.py:262 and timeLimit=20 on capital_capacity.py exist as honesty fuses, and at TPAC seed scale they never fire. At 50 sites × 500 SKUs × 52 weeks they would fire constantly — that's the scale at which the Gurobi conversation becomes real, and not before.
  • No IIS. When a model goes infeasible, CBC shrugs. We compensate structurally with elastic variables (§1.9), but a customer-facing "here are the three constraints that clash" needs either Gurobi's IIS or a hand-rolled elastic-everything diagnostic mode NOT BUILT.
  • No callbacks/lazy constraints. Rules out elegant formulations (e.g., subtour elimination on demand) — one reason VRP stays out of scope on this stack.
  • Numerics and presolve. CBC is more fragile on badly-scaled models. Our ₹-crore objective coefficients next to 0.01 fractions are exactly the kind of scaling Gurobi forgives and CBC sometimes doesn't. (Mitigation that costs nothing: keep money in ₹ thousands inside models — worth doing regardless of solver.)
The pragmatic upgrade ladder Step 1 (free, ~one line): swap CBC for HiGHS — PuLP supports HiGHS_CMD; expect strictly better solve times and numerics on the same model files. Zero formulation changes. Step 2 (free, new lane): add OR-Tools CP-SAT for the true-scheduling/reshuffle lane (§8) where MIP is the wrong hammer. Step 3 (commercial, evidence-gated): Gurobi only when a real customer instance produces a >30 s CBC/HiGHS solve or an unexplainable infeasibility — then the license pays for itself in IIS alone. Buying it today would be jewelry.

What "optimality" means in Gurobi's marketing — decoded

When their pages say a problem is solved "to optimality", the precise claim is: branch & bound closed the gap — incumbent = proven bound — so no better solution exists, mathematically. That's a real and valuable property (it converts "we think this plan is good" into "no consultant can ever find a better one under these assumptions"). Two honest footnotes vendors whisper: (a) optimal relative to the model — a wrong cost coefficient makes optimality worthless, which is why our UNITS/provenance obsession is not bureaucracy; (b) in practice everyone stops at gap ≤ 0.1–1% on hard instances, which is "optimal" the way "99.5% pure" is pure — fine, but it's a setting, not magic. Hexaly's counter-pitch is equally honest in the other direction: for problems where the proof is unreachable anyway, stop paying for bound-closing and buy solution quality per minute. Both are right, for different problems — see the taxonomy in §2 for which of ours is which.

5 · The unhinged free-view explicitly licensed opinions

You asked what I'd actually do next, viewpoints unfiltered, having built the thing. Here it is, ranked by conviction.

① The solver is a commodity. The explanation is the moat. Go all-in on that.

Building this system taught me something uncomfortable: every LP in it could be reproduced by a competent OR consultant in weeks, and CBC/HiGHS are free. What cannot be cheaply reproduced is the trust chain we accidentally built — seed badges, provenance chips, used-by graphs, gates that prove toggles are byte-inert, solvers that decline to act and say why. Mid-market India does not fail to adopt APS because the math is unavailable; it fails because the planner doesn't believe the number and the MD doesn't believe the planner. The product should be renamed in our own heads: not "a planning solver" but an explanation engine that happens to contain solvers. Every roadmap item should be scored "does this make a number more believable?" — and §6's decisions[] block becomes the single most important feature in the backlog.

② Ship the backtest, not the demo.

Unhinged version: stop polishing TPAC. The seed dataset's job was to keep us honest while building; it's done. The first real customer conversation should open with their CSV and end with "your 2025, re-planned: ₹‑X working capital, +Y% service — here's the week-by-week diff." That artifact converts skeptics because it's their data and a falsifiable claim. Everything in the app already supports it except the import mapper (gap #3) — which is why the mapper, the most boring item on the list, is secretly the highest-leverage one.

③ The disruption lane (§8) is the killer demo, and CP-SAT is its engine.

Every supply-chain pitch in the world shows a pretty plan. Almost none show Tuesday 7am: the crane is down and two welders didn't show — here are your three least-bad recoveries, costed. That's the moment a plant manager decides software is real. We are unusually close: rolling re-solve exists, branch fan-out exists (each recovery = a branch), the profit objective ranks them. The missing piece is the event model and a true intra-week scheduler — and that's CP-SAT, free. I would build §8 before any more planning depth. Depth we have; reflexes we don't.

④ Let an LLM drive the payloads — we accidentally built the perfect substrate.

Crazy-idea slot, decreasing craziness every month: the app exposes window.apiPost, typed payload builders, a solver-source endpoint (/api/meta/solver-source), and a provenance graph. That is precisely the tool surface an LLM agent needs to take "we're worried about a Chennai port strike in August" and turn it into pinned scenarios, branch fan-outs, and a ranked mitigation memo with every number carrying its provenance chip. The moat compounds: nobody can bolt explanation onto a black-box suite, but we can bolt language onto an explanation engine.

⑤ Things I'd change about our own past decisions (self-critique, no punches pulled)

  • 41 endpoints was depth-first vanity. Right call for learning, wrong shape for selling. I'd freeze the engine count today and spend the next equivalent effort on the six journeys of §7.
  • localStorage was a loan, and it's due. Every week of feature work raised the migration cost. Persistence should have landed around V3.
  • The transport mode-picker grew heuristic hair. Ranking + spike override + V5-4 splits each earned their place, but the combination is now three interacting special cases. If lanes/modes ever grow, rewrite as one small MILP (mode-assignment with split variables) — clearer semantics, and CBC would still solve it instantly. I kept it heuristic for explainability; I'd revisit that trade at the rewrite.
  • Weekly buckets will embarrass us in the disruption demo. "Crane down 9am–2pm" lands in a 168-hour bucket. Fine for the plan-of-record, wrong for reflexes — another reason §8 needs CP-SAT rather than more MILP.
  • We under-used our own duals. Aggregate surfaces shadow prices; nothing else does, and no UI turns one into a sentence a CFO would repeat. Cheap, high-trust, unbuilt.

7 · Six real-life scenarios, walked end-to-end through the app

Each scenario names the trigger, where you enter it, which engines fire in what order, what each one decides and why, and the shape of the outcome. ₹ figures tagged ◇ seed come from live runs against the TPAC dataset; the rest are representative. Scenarios 1 and 3's transport numbers were observed in this build's verification probes.

Scenario 1 — Festival spike: Diwali triples JX-450 demand with 4 weeks' notice

1
Demand tab. Distributor signals land as recent actuals; pattern_sensing cosine-matches the holiday ramp template and lifts weeks 0–6. You override the consensus rows for the spike months and — critically — pin them (V5-2b), so a routine re-forecast can't silently erase a human commitment. If the model's MAPE was already >30%, the V3-2 gate forces review before this consensus can commit at all.
2
Plan tab. aggregate.py re-solves: chase (hire+OT) vs build-ahead (carry inventory). Because holding cost is honest (carryRate × blended unit cost ÷ 12 ≈ ₹19/u/mo ◇ seed), it typically builds ahead 2 periods rather than hiring for a one-month peak — hire+fire events cost more than 8 weeks of holding. The shadow prices name which period's capacity binds; if P-peak shows a high dual, that's your OT-authorization memo, pre-written.
3
Production tab. The weekly MILP packs lots against the frozen fence; the changeover term batches JX-450 into longer runs; the V2-3 profit gate cross-checks the mix is still worth making at spike volumes.
4
Logistics tab. The spike flags the lane. Pre-V5-4 the solver flew everything; now _deadline_split flies only the consumption bridge. Observed in this build's canonical case: USE AIR → SPLIT FAST+SLOW, bridge 1,380 kg = (22d sea − 10 DoS) × 100 kg/day × 1.15 buffer, total ₹64,000 → ₹32,167 ◇ seed — half the panic-air bill, with the residual-stockout exposure priced into the comparison rather than ignored.
5
Scenarios tab. "⚡ Run all" fans out spike-softer/spike-harder branches server-side (V5-2); Monte Carlo with correlated demand–cost shocks (ρ=0.4) grades the committed plan's downside before you sign it.

Scenario 2 — Supplier shock: your bearing supplier cuts allocation 40% for two months

1
Sourcing tab. Enter the cut as the supplier's ₹/period capacity cap (V5-3). This is a constraint tightening — no code, no drama, one governed field.
2
Procurement engine. The MILP now can't serve requirements from the primary lane; overflow routes to the backup lane at its own MOQ and a +12% spot premium ◇ seed (premium_pct, UNITS-ledgered). Decisions you'll see: pull-forward POs inside the cap before the cut window (cheap), backup overflow only for the uncoverable residual (expensive, MOQ-rounded), and safety-stock floors— including any CVaR floor — kept intact rather than quietly raided.
3
Finance ripple. Landed cost on the backup lane reprices through the duty stack; effUnitCost moves; the margin chain (Q7/Q12) shows which FG SKUs just got thinner — sometimes the right answer is profitmix trimming a low-margin SKU for the quarter instead of paying premium bearings, and running both as branches settles it in numbers.
4
Promise check. Any open customer promise rides /api/solve/ctp again; if the displaced week moved, you find out now, not at ship date.

Scenario 3 — The impossible shipment: a 70-tonne project order, Delhi → Mumbai

1
The trap this build closed. No single mode accepts 70,000 kg (even an FCL-40 tops out at 26,700). The pre-V5-4 solver returned "no recommendation" and — the dangerous part — silently priced the lane at ₹0, so the total looked better the more unserveable your freight was. A green dashboard hiding a physical impossibility is the exact failure mode our "observed ≫ sampled ≫ reasoned" rule exists for.
2
With mode split armed, _capacity_split bin-packs full bulk loads + remainder: observed plan rail 60,000 + rail 10,000 = ₹93,000 ◇ seed, every leg deadline-checked, and the UI says the honest sentence: "no single mode fits 70,000 kg — split is the only plan" with saving: null because there is no single-mode baseline to save against. Honesty encoded in the schema, not the copy.

Scenario 4 — Cash crunch: working capital must shrink 20% this quarter

1
Finance tab. The real lever is the cost of money. capital_structure.py re-derives the source-weighted hurdle and min-WACC mix; a higher carry_rate (a FRACTION, per the ledger — the trap table exists because this exact number was once nearly mis-fed) flows outward as one governed token.
2
Everything downstream re-prices itself. EOQs shrink (√ of ordering/holding ratio), (s,S) bands tighten, Wagner–Whitin re-times orders, MEIO migrates buffers upstream — holding FG value just got dearer relative to quoting longer service, which is exactly the Graves–Willems trade — and the aggregate plan tilts from build-ahead toward chase. One input, eight engines, every move traceable to the same ₹ logic.
3
The honest counter-number. Monte Carlo shows the service-level cost of the leaner system; CVaR floors mark where the tail says "no further." You present the CFO a frontier — inventory ₹ vs P5 service — not a promise that thinner is free.

Scenario 5 — New SKU launch that eats its sibling: JX-600 vs JX-450

1
Products tab. New SKU with BOM, routing, cycle times; the changeover matrix auto-extends (V2-6). Set the cannibalization coefficient against JX-450.
2
Profitmix. The coupling constraint (q + cannib ≤ ceiling) makes the solver answer the only question that matters: incremental profit after cannibalization, not gross. If JX-600 is in the mix, it cleared that bar; if out, the §6 reduced-cost explanation says what margin or price would flip it.
3
Capacity reality. Routed hours land on shared lines; if utilisation pins, capital_capacity.py prices the expansion options against OT, and capital.py decides whether the tooling project beats the other uses of the CapEx ceiling — with the budget dual telling you what one more ₹ of budget would have earned.
4
Inventory layer. No history ⇒ forecastability badge flags it, MAPE gate watches the consensus, MEIO initially treats it as the τ=0 / MTO case until variance data earns it a buffer. The system's refusal to pretend it knows a launch curve is a feature.

Scenario 6 — Port congestion: Chennai inbound +10 days for six weeks

1
Network/Sourcing. Lead-time bump on affected lanes; risk.py has likely already flipped the cost series to HIGH-VOL regime, so the σ feeding safety stock is the stormy one, not the blended one.
2
MEIO + policy. ss = z·σ·√LT rises on the √ of the new lead time; reorder points jump immediately (the s in (s,S) is LT-demand + buffer). The pipeline-fill order this triggers is the expensive, correct, and usually-late-in-real-life move — here it's automatic.
3
Transport. Re-rank with +10d: marginal lanes flip sea→air on the deadline filter, and the V5-4 bridge logic keeps the flip surgical (fly the gap, sail the bulk). Customs days still count — the +4d import adder is in the transit math, the kind of detail congestion plans usually forget.
4
Branches. "6 weeks" is a guess; run 4/8/12-week branches concurrently and read where the plan changes shape (when does build-ahead beat premium freight?) — that boundary, not any single branch, is the decision.
What these six prove together The engines aren't a feature list — they're one pricing system seen from six angles: every scenario reduces to "a constraint moved; re-price everything it touches; show the cheapest honest response and what binds." That's the demo narrative, and every step above runs in the app today.

8 · When reality breaks the plan — disruption entry, tracking, and reshuffle DESIGN — NOT BUILT

Your exact scenarios: a required operator no-shows on line 1 or 2; a crane fails on a line; a schedule is missed. Today the app would absorb these only crudely (edit a capacity field, re-solve everything). Here is the design for doing it properly — built deliberately on three pieces that already exist: rolling re-solve with a frozen fence (/api/solve/rolling, frozen_weeks/shift_weeks at app.py:115–116), maintenance windows that already zero or scale line capacity (production.py maint_down/maint_scale), and concurrent branch fan-out (V5-2) to cost the alternatives.

8.1 The event model — one log, typed events, constraint deltas

// POST /api/events — append-only log; an event is a CONSTRAINT DELTA with a time window
{
  "id": "EV-2026-0612-01",
  "type": "operator_no_show",        // resource_out · machine_down · crane_down ·
                                     // material_late · lot_missed · rush_order · yield_bust
  "scope": { "line": "L1", "shift": "A" },
  "window": { "from": "2026-06-12T06:00", "to": "2026-06-12T14:00" },
  "delta": { "kind": "capacity_hours", "value": -8 },   // machine-translatable, always
  "severity": "medium",
  "status": "open",                  // open → mitigating → closed (with attribution)
  "note": "2 welders absent; agency called"
}

The non-negotiable design rule: every event type compiles to a constraint delta the solvers already understand. No-show ⇒ worker-hours off a line-shift (and via the V4-3 routed-hours model, the load reroutes correctly). Crane down ⇒ maint_scale on the window (the mechanism exists; only the entry path is missing). Material late ⇒ scheduled-receipt date shift. Missed lot ⇒ actual = 0 against plan, shortfall re-enters net requirements. Rush order ⇒ a CTP call with priority. New event types become data, not code.

8.2 Entry — three doors, zero forms-bureaucracy

  • Console "⚠ Report disruption" — type + scope + window, four taps. The Console is already the control tower (V3-5); this is its missing input side.
  • In-context flags — right-click a Gantt bar → "didn't run / ran short"; a PO row → "slipped to [date]". The screen where you see the plan is where you report its death.
  • API door — POST /api/events for the day when MES/attendance systems feed it (this is gap #8's execution loop growing its first root).

8.3 Re-solve semantics — pin the past, branch the recoveries

1
Pin history. Periods before now are facts: actual production, receipts, shipments become fixed constants (the frozen-fence machinery, pointed backward). A re-solve must never "fix" yesterday — pinned-past is what separates re-planning from revisionism.
2
Apply open event deltas to the affected windows only.
3
Fan out the recovery family as branches (the V5-2 engine, repurposed): absorb (re-optimize within remaining capacity — some orders slip), overtime (V4-3 per-shift OT caps say how much is even legal), reroute (eligibility already encodes which lines can run the SKU), expedite inbound/outbound (the V5-4 bridge logic prices partial expediting), renegotiate (CTP shows which promise slips cheapest). Each is one payload mutation; they solve concurrently.
4
Rank and narrate. Sort by the profit objective; render each as: ₹ cost vs incumbent · service hit (which orders move, by how long) · diff-vs-incumbent ("what moved" — computable from solutions we already diff in tests) · a one-line because, per §6's decisions[] contract. "Recovery B: +₹38k OT, zero promises missed, JX-300 W24 lot moves to L2 — because L2 had 11 slack hours and the changeover class matches."
5
Human picks; the event log records which recovery was taken — accountability and, later, training data for auto-suggesting recoveries.

8.4 Tracking and closure

  • Open-event banner on Console: count, age, ₹-at-risk (incumbent vs best-recovery delta — the cost of not deciding, updating live).
  • Attribution at closure: planned vs actual vs event-adjusted-plan splits every variance into disruption vs plan error — ending the weekly "why did we miss?" archaeology. This is the reconcile loop (reconcile.py) gaining a memory.
  • Pattern feedback: recurring no-shows on shift A are not events, they're a parameter — the log should eventually push effective-capacity corrections back into planning data (absenteeism factor per line-shift), the way pattern_sensing already learns demand shapes.

8.5 The honest solver note

Weekly buckets make same-day reshuffles coarse: "crane down 6 hours" becomes "this week has 6 fewer hours," which re-times lots, not the 2pm job. Costing recoveries — the decision that moves money — works correctly at that grain, and shipping §8.1–8.4 on the existing MILP is real value. But minute-level resequencing ("which job moves to 4pm") is disjunctive scheduling — the JSSP row marked NOT BUILT in §2 — and the right engine is OR-Tools CP-SAT (free, §3 step 2): interval variables for jobs, no-overlap per line, event windows as forbidden intervals, objective = weighted tardiness vs the incumbent so the reshuffle stays close to the plan people already walked the floor with. MILP for the plan-of-record, CP-SAT for the reflexes, one event log feeding both — that's the architecture, and per §5③ I'd build it next.

9 · Glossary — the twenty terms that make you sound like you've done this before

LP / MILPLinear program (continuous) / mixed-integer linear program. The integer part is what makes it hard.
Feasible regionAll variable values satisfying every constraint. Convex polytope for LPs; shattered lattice once integers enter.
Vertex optimalityA linear objective over a polytope peaks at a corner — the fact that makes LP fast and LP solutions extreme.
RelaxationThe same model with integrality dropped. Solves fast; bounds the true optimum; the skeleton of branch & bound.
Incumbent / gapBest integer solution found / its proven distance from the bound. Gap 0% = certificate of optimality.
Dual / shadow price (π)₹ value of relaxing a constraint by one unit. Zero on non-binding constraints. Invalid on MIPs without re-relaxing (§1.3).
Reduced costHow much a zero-valued variable's coefficient must improve before the solver uses it — "what price gets SKU-X into the mix."
BindingA constraint with zero slack at the optimum. The binding set is the diagnosis; the plan is just the symptom.
DegeneracyMultiple optima / over-determined vertices; why equally-good plans flicker between runs.
Big-MIndicator linking (q ≤ M·y). Keep M tight or branch & bound goes blind.
IISIrreducible infeasible subsystem — the minimal set of clashing constraints. Gurobi computes it; CBC can't; elastic variables are the free workaround.
Presolve / cutsModel shrinking and added valid inequalities before/during B&B — the actual technical content of commercial-solver superiority.
Warm startSeeding the solver with a known-good solution (e.g., yesterday's plan) — crucial for fast re-solves; CBC support is weak, another §3 ladder rung.
Newsvendor / critical fractileOptimal one-shot stocking = a cost ratio, not a service-level preference.
CVaRAverage of the worst (1−β) tail. Linear via Rockafellar–Uryasev; "robust" with an actual definition.
Guaranteed-service (GSM)The Graves–Willems MEIO frame: stages quote service times; buffers cover net replenishment time; τ=0 means MTO.
CLSPCapacitated lot-sizing problem — the correct name for a weekly bucketed production scheduler with setups (ours).
JSSP / disjunctive schedulingJob-level, machine-level, minute-level sequencing. CP-SAT territory, not MILP's.
Rolling horizon / frozen fenceRe-solve periodically; near-term decisions locked, far-term free. The backbone of both planning stability and §8's pinned past.
RecourseDecisions made after uncertainty resolves, inside the optimization (two-stage stochastic programming) — the rigorous upgrade our simulate-then-harden stack doesn't yet make.
