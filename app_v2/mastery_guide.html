Foundations — the optimization literacy everything else assumes

READ ONCE, USE EVERYWHERE

Ten of this app's engines are mathematical programs: they hand a model to the CBC solver (via the PuLP modeling library) and get back a provably best answer. Every one of them is built from exactly three parts. Learn the three parts once and every SOLVER tab below becomes readable.

The geometry — why "optimal" answers always sit in a corner

Linear constraints carve a flat-sided region (a polytope); a linear objective is a tilted plane over it. Push the plane as far as it goes and it last touches the region at a vertex — a corner where constraints intersect. Three consequences you will see all over this app: optimal plans are extreme (a line runs flat-out or not at all), they're locally stable (small data nudges don't move a vertex), and then suddenly jumpy (a big nudge flips to a different vertex entirely). Drag the price below and watch the optimum snap between corners — it never glides.

Duality — the solver tells you what capacity is worth

Every LP secretly solves a twin problem: price each constraint so the prices alone explain the optimum. Those prices are shadow prices (duals): the objective improvement from one more unit of a constraint's right-hand side. The rules to internalize:

Integers, branch & bound, and what "optimal" really claims

The moment a variable must be whole (workers, pallets, a yes/no machine purchase), the problem becomes a MILP and is solved by branch & bound: solve the relaxed LP, pick a fractional variable, split into two subproblems (x ≤ ⌊v⌋ / x ≥ ⌈v⌉), and prune any branch whose LP bound can't beat the best integer answer found so far. "Optimal" means the gap between best-found and best-possible closed to zero — a certificate, not a vibe. When a vendor says "near-optimal in seconds," ask what the gap was; a heuristic without a bound can't even tell you.

Where integers buy realism here: production.py integer lots + binary run/changeover flags · aggregate.py optional integer workforce · meio.py one-hot service-time selector · capital.py binary buy/lease · procurement.py MOQ steps. Each binary roughly doubles the worst-case tree — which is why every model here keeps its integer count lean.

Reading any solve — the 4-step discipline

1
Check status first. Optimal is a claim with a proof. Infeasible means your constraints contradict — the model is telling you about your factory, not about math. Several engines use elastic variables (priced slack like backorders or lost sales) so they degrade with an explanation instead of dying.
2
Watch what binds. The binding set IS the story of the plan. A plan where nothing binds is a plan you over-provisioned.
3
Mistrust the units, trust the ledger. yield_pct is a fraction despite the name; hold_pct is a percent; changeovers are hours in the UI and minutes at the API. The repo carries a units contract (UNITS.md) enforced by a lint — because unit bugs produce plausible wrong answers, the worst kind.
4
Ask "compared to what?" A ₹93,000 transport plan means nothing alone; vs the ₹-unserveable single-mode alternative it's the whole decision. The good engines here narrate the counterfactual; the VERDICT tabs call out the ones that don't.

Setup — Identity & Calendar

DEFINE
Who you legally are, when your factory works, and which engines you even see. Every solver downstream reads its clock and its tax/currency context from here.

Master this: configuration IS modeling

Setup looks administrative; it is not. Three of its numbers silently multiply through every plan: working calendar (work-days/week × net hours/shift × shifts = the capacity denominator every utilization figure divides by), service level (one α drives every safety-stock z-score in MEIO, policy sizing, and CVaR comparisons), and tax/currency regime (effective tax shapes the capital solver's after-tax NPVs; base currency + FX shapes landed cost). A wrong calendar is the most common way real planning deployments produce confidently wrong answers — the solvers are exact about the world you described.

The second concept is profile gating: the six onboarding answers (make policy, capacity tightness, imports, line count, distribution, external forecast) live here and gate whole engines. This is the app teaching a real lesson: a planning system should show a single-site domestic MTO shop a different product than a multi-site importer — same substrate, different exposed surface.

Products — Catalog, BOM & Costs

DEFINE
What you make, what it's made of, and what it truly costs. The margin every optimizer maximizes is manufactured here — get cost wrong and every "optimal" mix is optimally wrong.

Master this: the three costs and the one BOM

Every planning system worth using maintains a typed cost chain, and this app makes it explicit: p.cost (direct material+labor) ⊂ effUnitCost (the ONE blended truth used for holding/margin everywhere) ⊂ landed cost (adds duty stack + freight + FX, computed in Sourcing). Master the discipline: any time you see a margin, ask which cost it nets against. Mixing chain levels is the classic spreadsheet sin this design exists to kill.

The bill of materials is the other spine: finished good → parts → quantities-per. MRP (Sourcing) is just this tree times the schedule, offset by lead times. And yield belongs to the product master, not the schedule: if only 95% of started units come out good, the planner's real choice is "start more" — and the cost of that choice (extra starts + rework ₹/failed unit) must be priced into the optimization, not discovered afterwards. That's exactly what V4-6 wired in.

Network — Nodes, Lanes, Contracts & On-Hand

DEFINE
Where material physically sits and moves: plants, warehouses, suppliers, customers; storage classes; time-varying contracts; and the opening on-hand ledger that every netting calculation starts from.

Master this: one ledger, many readers

The deepest idea on this tab is the on-hand ledger. Ask "how much stock do we have?" and a naive system answers with one number; a real one asks where and of what scope. The ledger here is a node×item matrix, and downstream solvers read it through a scoped lens: production's opening FG inventory and procurement's init_inventory both come from onHandFor() — FG scoped by a governed netFgScope (plant+warehouse by default, the DRP convention), RM at plant+warehouse. Multi-site netting (V5-1) means a warehouse full of finished goods correctly reduces what the plant schedules.

Second idea: contracts are time-varying prices, not static fields. A supplier contract that steps from ₹118 to ₹126 in March belongs in a ledger the procurement solver can read per-period — buying ahead of a price step is one of the genuinely profitable moves an optimizer finds that humans systematically miss.

A BUG WORTH REMEMBERING Before V5-1, procurement silently fabricated any part's opening inventory as avg-demand × (lead-time+1) when the key was absent — a friendly default that quietly invented stock. The ledger send exists to kill that path. Lesson for any planning system: defaults that invent material are bugs wearing helpful faces.

04

Demand — History, Forecast, ABC/XYZ & Pins

PLAN
The single most leveraged stage: every downstream solver optimizes against the number produced here. An exact optimizer fed a 40%-wrong forecast is a precision instrument aimed at the wrong target.

Master this: forecasting as a competition you referee, not a model you marry

1 · Decompose before you model. Any demand series is level + trend + seasonality + noise. Eyeball the TPAC history chart and name each component; if you can't see seasonality by eye, an algorithm claiming to model it is probably fitting noise. The fraction of variance that is noise sets the ceiling on any forecast — the goal is never zero error, it's error no worse than the noise floor, with known bias.

2 · Hold out or be fooled. In-sample fit is flattery: a high-order model fits history perfectly and forecasts garbage. The only honest score is on data the model never saw — train on all but the last N periods, score on those N. Everything in this tab is ranked by holdout MAPE, never in-sample fit. Internalize the asymmetry: a simple model that scores 12% holdout beats a sophisticated one at 11.8% in-sample every time.

3 · Know the model families and when each wins. Naive-seasonal ("same as last year, same week") — the baseline everything must beat, and embarrassingly often doesn't get beaten. Holt-Winters (add/mult) — explicit level/trend/season smoothing; wins on clean seasonal series with limited history. ARIMA — autocorrelation structure; wins when demand momentum matters. Tree ensembles (RF/GB/XGBoost) — win when there are regressors (price, promotions, macro indices) or nonlinear calendar effects; lose on short history. MLP — same regime, needs even more data. Hybrid (HW + ML on residuals) — stats carries the seasonal skeleton, ML mops up what's left; often the quiet winner.

4 · Measure like an adult. MAPE for comparability, but know its flaw (explodes near zero demand); bias separately, because a 10%-MAPE forecast that's always 10% low stocks you out forever; FVA — forecast value-add — your touch-ups versus the statistical baseline: if human overrides make the forecast worse (they usually do, optimism is occupational), FVA shows it, with receipts.

5 · Forecast ≠ plan. The forecast is an input. The consensus + override + pin layer is where the business commits — and committed numbers must survive a re-forecast (here, pins do — V5-2b). Separating the two is the whole point of S&OP.

Plan — S&OP Aggregate, Level-vs-Chase & Duals

PLAN
The month-grain capacity bargain: how much to produce, with how many people, holding how much, backordering what. The first true MILP in the user's path — and the tab where shadow prices become business language.

Master this: the level-vs-chase bargain, priced

Aggregate planning answers one question: when demand is seasonal, do you flex to follow it (chase) or hold steady and buffer with inventory (level)? Chase pays in hiring/firing/overtime; level pays in holding cost and backorder risk. Every consultant chart shows the two as a binary. The MILP's answer is better: it prices both and lands wherever the mix is cheapest — usually a hybrid no chart would have drawn. Your job shifts from choosing a strategy to auditing the prices: if the solver chases harder than feels right, your hire/fire costs are probably understated, not the solver wrong.

Two subtleties this app gets right that most courses skip. (a) The planning unit is labor-weighted, not physical. Adding crankshaft bearings to piston rings is meaningless; the aggregate unit here is demand-weighted by labor content (cycle × hands-on %), so "capacity" means worker-time, which is the thing that's actually scarce. The headline UI still shows physical units; the conversion is owned by one function. (b) Cost mode vs profit mode. Classic aggregate planning minimizes cost assuming all demand must be met. The V4-1 profit objective adds lost-sales variables priced at margin — now the solver may choose to walk away from peak demand when serving it costs more than it earns. That single modeling change turns a feasibility exercise into a business decision.

Production — Lines, MPS, Sequencing & ATP/CTP

PLAN
The month-plan becomes a week-by-week, line-by-line schedule with real lots, real changeovers, real yield — and a promise engine that can tell a customer "yes, by week 9" with a straight face.

Master this: lot-sizing is the schedule; sequencing is a repair

Be precise about what problem class this is, because vendors blur it. This engine solves capacitated lot-sizing (CLSP, big-bucket): each week is a bucket; the solver picks how much of each SKU each line runs that week, paying setup when a SKU appears. What it does not decide is the minute-level order of jobs within the week — that's job-shop scheduling (JSSP), a genuinely different and harder problem (interval variables, no-overlap, makespan), and this app honestly doesn't have it. Instead, a separate engine (sequencing.py) repairs the within-week order after the fact: exact brute force at ≤8 SKUs (all 40,320 orders evaluated), nearest-neighbour + node-reinsertion above. Two-stage decomposition — optimal lots, then good-enough order — is how most real APS systems work too; the difference is whether they tell you.

The second mastery item is the fence. A schedule that reshuffles itself every morning is worse than a slightly stale one — the shop floor needs commitments. The frozen/slushy fence (from Setup) means re-plans hold weeks 1–2 fixed, let 3–4 change with damping, and free everything beyond. The rolling re-solve (app.py rolling, frozen_weeks) is the app's heartbeat — and the substrate the future disruption lane builds on.

Third: promising. ATP (available-to-promise) is arithmetic — uncommitted supply per week, cumulative. CTP (capable-to-promise, V4-5) is a look-ahead solve: "if I insert this order, what's the earliest week the schedule still closes?" The difference between a sales tool and a planning tool is exactly the difference between those two answers.

Sourcing — MRP, Procurement MILP, Lot-Sizing & MEIO

PLAN
The schedule explodes into material: what to buy, when, from whom, in what lots, with which safety buffers — under budgets, MOQs, supplier capacity, and lead-time ladders.

Master this: four nested decisions, four different mathematics

1 · MRP is bookkeeping, not optimization. BOM × schedule, offset by lead time, netted against on-hand and receipts. Deterministic arithmetic — master the netting identity (Network section) and the lead-time ladder (order this week for material needed in week+LT) and you know MRP.

2 · Lot-sizing is a real optimization with a famous exact answer. Given time-varying needs, ordering cost per PO, and holding cost: EOQ assumes flat demand (wrong but robust); period-order-quantity, Silver-Meal, least-unit-cost are heuristics; Wagner-Whitin is the exact dynamic program — O(T²), trivial at this scale, and this app ships it among 13 policies with an auto-selector that just evaluates all of them and takes the cheapest. When ordering cost is high and demand lumpy, WW beats EOQ by double digits; when demand is flat they tie — run the auto-select and read which regime you're in.

3 · Safety stock is a service-level contract with variance. σ of demand over lead time × z(α) — but the master move is where to hold it: MEIO (multi-echelon inventory optimization) decides per node, and the Graves–Willems insight is that a node quoting zero service time to its downstream passes risk along; quote longer and you hold it yourself. The MILP picks the cheapest risk placement across the whole network.

4 · Procurement under constraint is where it all collides: budgets per period, MOQs, supplier capacity caps (₹/period, V5-3), backup lanes at a premium, price steps from contracts. That's the procurement MILP — the one engine consuming all three stages above it.

Logistics — Transport, Allocation & Mode Split

PLAN
Moving the plan: which mode carries which shipment, how DC→customer allocation minimizes cost, when loads consolidate, and when a deadline forces splitting one shipment across fast and slow modes.

Master this: mode choice is a deadline-and-density problem

Every shipment lives on two axes: how soon it must land and how heavy/dense it is. Sea is ~10× cheaper than air per kg and ~5× slower; rail and road sit between. The naive policy — "urgent → air, else sea" — wastes money at both ends, and the two failure cases bound the whole domain: capacity binds (the load physically doesn't fit one mode's vehicle/container — a 40' sea container caps near 26,700 kg) and deadline binds (sea lands after the customer's date). The master move for a deadline bind is the bridge split: compute the days of stock you'll be short before the slow mode lands, fly only that bridge quantity (× a safety buffer), and ship the rest cheap. It routinely beats all-air by 40–60%. The app's V5-4 engine does both splits, and only when a bind actually exists — armed but unbound solves return byte-identical to disarmed ones, by design and by test.

Second concept: allocation — the classic transportation LP (the problem LP was practically invented for): which DC serves which customer so total ₹·km is minimal, subject to supply at each DC and demand at each customer. It's the one model here whose optimal basis has a beautiful structure (a spanning tree of lanes) — visible in how few lanes the solution actually uses.

Scenarios — Monte Carlo, CVaR & Resilience

DECIDE
The committed plan meets uncertainty: simulate correlated shocks, read the tail not the average, size buffers against the bad days, and stress the plan to its breaking point on purpose.

Master this: averages lie, tails decide, correlation is the multiplier

1 · The flaw of averages: a plan optimized against mean demand is not the plan with mean performance — costs are asymmetric (a stockout costs more than the equivalent overstock saves), so the expected cost of a plan under uncertainty is always worse than its cost at expected demand. Simulation exists to measure that gap.

2 · Newsvendor is the atom of stochastic inventory: with underage cost Cu (margin lost) and overage Co (holding/salvage loss), the optimal stocking quantile is the critical fractile Cu/(Cu+Co). Every service-level decision is this equation wearing different clothes.

3 · CVaR is the grown-up objective: VaR says "the 95th-percentile loss is X"; CVaR says "and the average of the worst 5% is Y" — coherent, convex, and (the Rockafellar–Uryasev result) minimizable with a plain LP. Optimizing CVaR instead of expected cost buys insurance: slightly worse average, much better worst days.

4 · Correlation is what kills diversification: if demand surges and input costs spike together (ρ>0 — they do; both ride the same boom), your bad scenarios are badder than independent sampling pretends. The V4-7 engine shocks demand and cost through a Cholesky factor with default ρ ◇0.4 — remove that and the tail thins dishonestly.
