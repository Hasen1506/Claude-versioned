STEP 01

Setup · Parameter Governance

"Before any solver runs, every downstream decision needs to know what world it's operating in."

Core Concept

Imagine every team using a different interest rate, a different exchange rate, and a different service-level target. Each decision looks locally rational but the plans contradict each other. This is the default state of most supply chains — and what Step 1 prevents.

The REGISTRY is a single, versioned store for every economic assumption. But storing parameters centrally isn't enough — the real work is dependency lineage with cascade: when you edit invHoldingSpread, every solver that reads it must immediately flag as STALE, blocking old outputs until re-solved. Without cascade, the database looks like a single source of truth but silently feeds stale arithmetic to live decisions.

  • serviceLevel 0.95 — governs safety stock z-score in Sourcing (Step 6) and MEIO (Step 7)
  • invHoldingSpread 12.8% — added to WACC to form carryRate used in procurement economics
  • fxRates {USD 84.20, EUR 91.40} — converts landed cost for every multi-currency PO (Step 6)
  • 6-day week · frozen/slushy horizon — defines working-day buckets every time-phase anchors to

STEP 02

Demand · Forecasting & Sensing

"Every other step optimises around demand. If demand is wrong, everything downstream is systematically wrong."

Core Concept

Demand is never one thing. A product's sales contain a baseline trend, a seasonal pattern, a promotional pulse, a new-product ramp, and random noise. No single forecasting algorithm handles all components well. The job of this step is to pick the right model per SKU from a competition and produce a committed forecast the rest of the plan builds on.

Holt-Winters exponential smoothing models trend and seasonality by weighting recent history more heavily — fast, transparent, robust for mature products. Random Forest treats demand as a supervised ML problem: promo flags, price, day-of-week, and calendar are features; it catches nonlinear relationships Holt-Winters can't. Croston's method is the specialist for intermittent demand — items that sell 0 units for 8 weeks, then 50, then 0. It separately models when demand will occur and how much.

The MASE/MAPE backtest decides the winner per SKU: hold back the last N periods, run all models, measure out-of-sample error, crown the lowest-error model. The output is a committed demand signal — an explicit event, not a background auto-update — because downstream solvers need a stable signal to optimise against. The prediction interval σ feeds directly into Step 9's risk simulations.

Three models compete per SKU. The MASE backtest on held-out periods crowns the winner. The PI band (shaded) becomes Step 9's uncertainty input.

STEP 03

Plan · Aggregate S&OP

"Should we level the workforce and hold buffer inventory, or chase demand with hiring cycles? This LP finds the cheapest hybrid."

Core Concept

Before scheduling a single production run, you must answer a strategic workforce question. Two pure strategies exist: Level production — keep the workforce constant, build inventory in slow periods and draw it down at peaks. The cost is inventory holding. Chase demand — hire when demand rises, fire or cut overtime when it falls, match production to demand exactly. The cost is labour volatility: hire/fire fees, training lag, and morale impact.

Neither extreme is cheapest in practice. The Hax–Meal LP finds the optimal hybrid by minimising total cost across the horizon: labour holding + inventory holding + hire/fire fees + overtime premium + backorder penalty. It outputs a period-by-period workforce size and production volume — the aggregate plan.

The critical nuance: this LP works in labour-weighted units, not physical units. A SKU taking 3 worker-hours counts 3× the capacity load of a 1-hour SKU. The reconciliation (physical 1,260 ≠ labour-weighted 1,124) is where errors silently enter. Every downstream step must know which basis it's using and convert correctly. The allow_backorder flag is a key lever — enabling it may flip infeasible peak periods to feasible by absorbing them as planned stockouts.

The LP finds the cost-minimising blend — neither pure level nor pure chase — given actual hire/fire/OT/holding/backorder costs.

STEP 04

Production · Schedule & Bottleneck

"The aggregate plan says how much. The schedule says which machine, which sequence, and when — and exposes the single constraint governing everything else."

Core Concept

The aggregate plan hands down a volume target: "make 4,000 units of SKU-mix in Q1." Production scheduling converts this into a physical Gantt: LINE-01 runs SKU-A from Monday 6am to Tuesday 2pm, then a changeover to SKU-B, then LINE-02 runs SKU-C in parallel. This is where the Theory of Constraints (TOC) becomes central.

TOC's key insight: in any system, one resource limits total throughput — the bottleneck. Improving any non-bottleneck resource wastes money and doesn't move the output needle. Every management action should focus on: (1) identify the bottleneck, (2) exploit it — zero idle time, no quality defects that consume its capacity, (3) subordinate everything else to feeding it, (4) only then elevate it by buying more. In this system, LINE-01 is the bottleneck. Every idle minute on it is revenue permanently lost — the clock never rewinds.

Changeover minimisation is equally critical. The 6×6 changeover matrix gives the time and cost to switch between every pair of SKUs. The Hamiltonian sequencing solver finds the SKU order that minimises the total changeover cost — a Travelling Salesman variant. The output projected_inventory is the moment-by-moment stock level as production builds and demand draws down. This is not just operational — it becomes the EVA capital base Finance charges carrying cost against in Step 11.

LINE-01 is the bottleneck. Changeover gaps (orange slivers) are throughput permanently lost. projected_inventory feeds Finance's EVA capital base in Step 11.

STEP 05

Profit Mix · The Glass-Box LP

"Given the bottleneck and demand ceilings, which SKU combination maximises contribution margin — and why? The dual variables tell the whole story."

Core Concept

Step 4 found the bottleneck: LINE-01 has a fixed number of machine-hours available. Step 5 asks: given that constraint, which mix of the 6 SKUs maximises total profit? This is the classic throughput accounting / product-mix LP — and it has a precise, auditable answer.

The feasible region is all SKU-volume combinations satisfying: (1) LINE-01 capacity constraint — total machine-hours used ≤ available, (2) demand ceiling per SKU — can't sell more than customers want, (3) non-negativity. The LP finds the vertex of this region that maximises total contribution margin (price minus variable cost, summed across all SKUs).

The real value is in the dual variables. The shadow price on LINE-01: if you had one more machine-hour, how much extra profit would you make? This is the exact economic value of capacity expansion — and it must equal what Finance uses in Step 11 to justify a CapEx. The reduced cost on TPA-7722: it's negative, meaning every unit you make of it destroys value at the margin by consuming LINE-01 time that could produce something more profitable. The crossover price shows what price TPA-7722 would need to re-earn its place. This "glass box" LP explanation is the product's core wedge — IBP and o9 hide the dual variables entirely.

The LP finds the optimal vertex. The shadow price on LINE-01 equals the financial value of capacity expansion — and must match Finance's CapEx model in Step 11.

STEP 06

Sourcing · Procurement & BOM

"Now that you know what you'll make, buy the right materials at the right time — no earlier (capital cost), no later (stockout)."

Core Concept

Production planning says "make 800 units of SKU-A in week 4." Procurement works backwards: which raw materials does SKU-A require (BOM explosion), how long do they take to arrive (lead time), how much uncertainty exists in that lead time and in demand (safety stock), and what lot size is economical given ordering cost vs carrying cost (EOQ / MOQ).

The per-FG BOM is critical: not the master BOM (which may include inactive components or default routings), but the BOM for the specific planned production run, accounting for actual scrap rates and substitution rules. The BOM check (SR-1) asserts that the buy plan explodes only the components the selected FG uses — nothing more, nothing less. This matters every time you change the product mix, because components unique to a dropped SKU must fall out of the purchase plan automatically.

Safety stock sizing uses one of two methods per part: Heizer (SS = z × σ_LTD) is fast and transparent, assuming normally distributed lead-time demand. CVaR (Rockafellar–Uryasev) sizes the buffer to cover the worst 5% of demand scenarios — more conservative, appropriate for fat-tailed or lumpy demand. The governing parameter is carryRate = WACC + spread: higher carrying cost pushes the optimum toward less inventory and lower service, which is exactly why carryRate must cascade from Step 1 when WACC changes. If it doesn't, procurement is optimising against the wrong cost of money.

BOM explosion → per-component lead-time PO plan → SS sized by service level. carryRate must update whenever WACC changes in Step 1.

STEP 07

Inventory · MEIO & Pooling

"The √N law: pool N independent buffers and you need only √N as much safety stock for the same service. Where you hold inventory is as important as how much."

Core Concept

Step 6 sized safety stock at a single echelon. Multi-Echelon Inventory Optimisation (MEIO) asks a deeper question: at which level of the supply chain should safety stock be held? Raw materials are cheap (low unit cost) but far from the customer — a stockout at the RM level takes weeks to recover. Finished goods are expensive but can reach the customer next day. Holding inventory upstream reduces capital cost but increases response time. This is a genuine tradeoff, and the right answer varies per part and per echelon.

The Graves–Willems Guaranteed Service Model (GSM) optimises this tradeoff: given the supply chain graph with lead times and holding costs at each node, it finds the commitment lead times each echelon promises to the next that minimise total safety stock cost while guaranteeing the service level. Echelons with short replenishment times don't need buffers; the buffer migrates to echelons where lead times are long or holding costs are low.

The Square-Root Pooling Law is the headline: if you pool N independent, identically distributed demand streams into one location, required safety stock grows only with √N, not N. Four DCs each holding independent safety stock can be consolidated (or virtually pooled) into one location holding √4 = 2× less. The "capital freed" number — Σ(decentralised SS) minus Σ(pooled SS) expressed in currency — is real working capital released without reducing service level.

Pooling N demand streams reduces required safety stock to √N × individual. The freed capital is real working capital released without cutting service level.

Result: 8–15% more precise capital placement — less SS where the upstream is reliable, more where it's variable — without changing the headline service level. The tradeoff is computational complexity: stochastic-service MEIO is NP-hard for large networks and typically requires decomposition heuristics.

STEP 08

Logistics · Transportation

"Making the product and having it in the right place are two different problems. This step solves the second one."

Core Concept

The production plan says what will be made and when. Logistics answers: how does it flow from factory to distribution centres to customers, at what cost, and in what quantities? This is the classic transportation LP — one of the oldest and most well-understood problems in operations research.

The transportation LP minimises total shipping cost subject to two conservation constraints: supply constraints (no DC can receive more than the factory produces) and demand constraints (each customer zone receives exactly what it needs). The output is an allocation plan — which factory serves which DCs, which DCs serve which demand zones, and what volume flows on each lane. A key practical output is consolidation opportunities: small shipments from the same origin to nearby destinations can be grouped into full truckload moves, reducing per-unit freight cost significantly.

This step is explicitly the simplest layer in the framework — a single-echelon, single-mode, static transportation model. It assumes a truck is a truck, all lanes are available at any time, and there are no delivery time windows. A real logistics optimisation would model multi-leg routes (factory → cross-dock → DC → customer), multiple modes (truck vs rail vs air vs sea), time windows (delivery must arrive by Thursday 4pm), and carrier capacity constraints. The gap is acknowledged — this tab is marked "lighter" and flagged for future domain review.

STEP 09

Risk · Monte Carlo & Stress

"The committed plan is a single point. Reality is a distribution. This step measures how good the plan is at the edges — where it survives, where it breaks."

Core Concept

Steps 1–8 produced a committed plan built around point estimates: the forecast mean, the expected lead time, the agreed production schedule. But the real world is probabilistic — demand will be higher or lower than forecast, supplier lead times will vary, equipment will occasionally fail. The committed plan represents exactly one scenario out of thousands of possible futures.

Monte Carlo simulation generates 10,000 scenarios where demand is drawn from its historical distribution (parameterised by the PI band σ from Step 2), lead times vary around their historical mean and variance, and the production schedule executes with its current Gantt — not a re-optimised plan. This distinction is critical: the simulation runs against the committed production Gantt cached from Step 4, not a fresh optimisation under each scenario. The result is a stress test of the actual plan, not the best possible plan in each scenario. This is control-tower testing, not planning-as-optimism.

CVaR95 (Conditional Value at Risk at 95%) is the tail risk metric: in the worst 5% of simulated scenarios, what is the average fill rate? A plan that achieves 97% average service but 60% fill in its worst 5% of scenarios is not a robust plan. The ResilienceStress function ramps a parameter one step at a time (demand +10%, +20%, +30%...) and re-runs the full MC at each level, producing a degradation curve: "this plan survives to +25% demand increase, but breaks — fill rate drops below 85% — at +35%."

The distribution shows fill-rate outcomes across 10,000 scenarios. CVaR95 is the average of the worst 5%. The committed plan line shows where reality anchors in the distribution.

STEP 10

What-If · Concurrent Cockpit

"Explore hypotheticals freely — demand spike, FX shock, supplier delay — without touching the live plan. The branch disappears cleanly; the live plan is byte-identical after."

Core Concept

A planner managing a live supply chain needs to answer hypotheticals every day: "What if our main supplier delays 2 weeks? What if we get a surprise +20% demand spike from a new contract? What if the USD/INR rate moves 5 points?" The challenge is exploring these questions without corrupting the live plan — the plan that production, procurement, and finance are currently executing against.

The what-if mechanism works in four steps: (1) Clone the current plan state — all solver inputs, all cached results, all committed values. (2) Perturb — change demand by +20%, or change an FX rate, or delay a supplier's lead time. (3) Re-solve only the affected solvers in the right dependency order: demand change → re-solve production → re-solve sourcing → re-solve finance. Unaffected solvers (logistics, if the demand change is small) can keep their current results. (4) Byte-restore the live working set after the branch — the live plan is returned to its original state, bit-for-bit identical.

The concurrency guarantee is the hardest part: the live working set must be untouched during the what-if computation. This is the supply chain analog of a git branch — you can make any changes on the branch, compare the results, and merge or discard them, without affecting main. Kinaxis RapidResponse does this server-side with true concurrent versioning — multiple planners can branch simultaneously. The current implementation approximates this client-side, which works for single-user analysis but doesn't scale to concurrent multi-planner branching.

Observability Assert
Before what-if: checksum the live working set. After byte-restore: re-checksum. Assert equality. If they differ, the restore is incomplete — the live plan has been modified by the branch. This is the most critical operational guarantee in the what-if system.
Gap: Client-Side Only — Not Server-Side Concurrent at Scale

The current implementation approximates branching client-side: it works for single-user, sequential analysis. When two planners simultaneously create branches from the same live plan (one testing a demand shock while another tests a supplier delay), there is no server-side state management to prevent them from overwriting each other's branches or corrupting the live plan.

If you implemented true server-side concurrent planning (Kinaxis-style): the server maintains a version graph of plan states, like git. Each planner works on their own branch simultaneously. Branches can be compared, merged (if non-conflicting), or flagged for human resolution (if conflicting). This enables true collaborative planning where a Sales team branch and a Supply team branch can run in parallel and be reconciled in an S&OP meeting. Required infrastructure: event-sourced state management with CRDT or OT conflict resolution. Non-trivial to build, but the payoff for multi-user deployments is significant.
